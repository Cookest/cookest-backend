# Chapter 1: Macronutrient Metabolism, Enzymatic Digestion, and Dietary Reference Intakes

Macronutrients—carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids—provide the structural substrates and chemical energy required to maintain human physiological homeostasis. The study of these compounds requires a detailed understanding of their biochemical structures, their mechanical and enzymatic pathways of digestion, and the systemic networks that regulate their absorption, transport, and metabolic disposal.

## Carbohydrate digestion and glucose homeostasis

Carbohydrates represent the primary source of metabolic fuel for the human body, with the central nervous system relying on glucose as its obligate energy substrate under non-starvation conditions. The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) of $130\text{ g/day}$ for adults and children is established based on the minimum glucose consumption required by the brain. The Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Range (AMDR) for carbohydrates is $45\%$ to $65\%$ of total daily energy intake, primarily sourced from low-energy-density and low-glycemic-index whole grains, fruits, and vegetables.

The digestion of carbohydrates begins in the oral cavity. Salivary glands secrete salivary amylase, which initiates the hydrolytic cleavage of $\alpha\text{-(1,4)-glucosidic}$ bonds in starches, converting complex polysaccharides into maltose and oligosaccharides. This chemical digestion halts in the highly acidic gastric environment of the stomach, which denatures salivary amylase.

As gastric contents empty into the duodenum, the presence of acid and nutrients triggers hormonal signals. The duodenum releases secretin, which activates the pancreas to secrete bicarbonate to neutralize stomach acid. Simultaneously, the duodenum releases cholecystokinin (CCK) to trigger the secretion of pancreatic enzymes, and Gastric Inhibitory Peptide (GIP), which acts in a feedback loop to inhibit gastrin production in the stomach and increase the cellular uptake of glucose. Pancreatic $\alpha$-amylase continues starch hydrolysis in the small intestine, breaking down remaining polysaccharides into disaccharides and oligosaccharides.

The final phase of carbohydrate digestion occurs at the brush border of the small intestinal enterocytes. Microvillar membrane-bound disaccharidases—specifically maltase, sucrase, and lactase—hydrolyze disaccharides into their constituent monosaccharide units. Maltase breaks down maltose into two glucose molecules; sucrase cleaves sucrose into glucose and fructose; and lactase hydrolyzes lactose into glucose and galactose.

These monosaccharides are absorbed across the apical membrane of the enterocyte via active transport (sodium-glucose cotransporter 1, SGLT1, for glucose and galactose) and facilitated diffusion (glucose transporter 5, GLUT5, for fructose), before exiting the basolateral membrane via GLUT2 into the portal circulation for transport to the liver.

## Protein digestion, nitrogen disposal, and the urea cycle

Proteins serve structural, enzymatic, and hormonal roles throughout the body. The updated federal guidelines recommend a daily protein intake of $1.2\text{ to }1.6\text{ g/kg}$ of body weight, representing a significant shift from the historical standard of $0.8\text{ g/kg}$ of body weight to support optimal muscle preservation and metabolic health. The corresponding AMDR for protein spans $10\%$ to $35\%$ of daily energy intake.

Protein digestion is initiated in the stomach. Gastric parietal cells secrete hydrochloric acid, which lowers the pH of the gastric lumen, denaturing folded proteins and exposing their peptide bonds. Acidic conditions activate the zymogen pepsinogen (secreted by chief cells) into the active endopeptidase pepsin, which cleaves polypeptides into smaller oligopeptide fragments.

Upon entering the duodenum, the zymogen activation cascade is triggered. Enteropeptidase on the brush border activates pancreatic trypsinogen into trypsin, which subsequently activates chymotrypsin, elastase, and carboxypeptidases. These enzymes, alongside brush-border peptidases, hydrolyze oligopeptides into free amino acids, dipeptides, and tripeptides for absorption.

Amino acid catabolism requires the safe disposal of toxic nitrogenous waste. The first phase involves transamination, catalyzed by pyridoxal-5'-phosphate (PLP, Vitamin B6)-dependent aminotransferases, principally alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST). These enzymes transfer the $\alpha$-amino group of an amino acid to $\alpha$-ketoglutarate, yielding glutamate and a corresponding $\alpha$-keto acid. ALT and AST promote these reversible reactions in the cytosol and mitochondria.

All amino acids undergo transamination except lysine, threonine, proline, and hydroxyproline, which bypass this step and undergo direct deamination. Non-oxidative deamination of hydroxy amino acids (serine, threonine, and homoserine) is catalyzed by PLP-dependent dehydratases (e.g., serine-threonine dehydratase), converting serine to pyruvate and threonine to $\alpha$-ketobutyrate while directly releasing free ammonium ($NH_4^+$).

Vigorously contracting skeletal muscles generate large amounts of pyruvate and lactate from glycolysis, as well as ammonia from protein breakdown. To transport this nitrogen safely to the liver, muscle tissue utilizes pyruvate as an amine acceptor to form alanine via transamination with glutamate. The glucose-alanine cycle transports nitrogen from muscles to the liver in a non-toxic form. In the liver, ALT acts in reverse, transferring the amino group from alanine back to $\alpha$-ketoglutarate, forming pyruvate for gluconeogenesis and glutamate. Glutamate is then transported into the mitochondria, where it undergoes oxidative deamination via glutamate dehydrogenase, releasing free ammonium and regenerating $\alpha$-ketoglutarate.

The toxic ammonium is converted into non-toxic, water-soluble urea via the irreversible, energy-consuming urea cycle in the liver. The rate-limiting step of the urea cycle is catalyzed by mitochondrial Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase I (CPS-I), which requires N-acetylglutamate as an obligate allosteric activator. This step condenses $NH_4^+$ and $HCO_3^-$ using $2\text{ ATP}$ to form carbamoyl phosphate. Carbamoyl phosphate is then transferred to ornithine by ornithine transcarbamoylase (OTC), yielding citrulline, which is exported to the cytosol.

In the cytosol, argininosuccinate synthetase condenses citrulline with aspartate—which provides the second nitrogen atom for urea—consuming $1\text{ ATP}$ (hydrolyzed to $AMP$ and pyrophosphate, $PPi$, equivalent to the expenditure of $2\text{ ATP}$ bonds) to form argininosuccinate. Argininosuccinate lyase cleaves argininosuccinate into fumarate and arginine. Fumarate can enter the citric acid cycle, while arginase 1 hydrolyzes arginine to yield urea and regenerate ornithine, which is shuttled back into the mitochondria to restart the cycle. The overall cycle consumes four high-energy phosphate bonds ($3\text{ ATP} \rightarrow 2\text{ ADP} + \text{AMP} + \text{PPi}$). Defects in any of these pathway enzymes result in clinical hyperammonemia, a neurotoxic state that severely impairs central nervous system function.

## Lipid digestion and essential fatty acid requirements

Dietary lipids represent a concentrated energy source and are essential for cellular membrane structure and steroid hormone synthesis. The AMDR for total dietary fat is established at $20\%$ to $35\%$ of daily energy, with saturated fat consumption restricted to less than $10\%$ of daily calories to minimize cardiovascular disease risk.

Lipid digestion begins minimally in the stomach via acid-stable lingual and gastric lipases, which hydrolyze short- and medium-chain triglycerides. The bulk of lipid digestion occurs in the duodenum. Bile salts synthesized by the liver and stored in the gallbladder are released into the duodenum to emulsify large lipid droplets into micro-micelles, drastically increasing the surface area for enzymatic attack. Pancreatic lipase, facilitated by its co-factor colipase, hydrolyzes triglycerides into free fatty acids and 2-monoglycerides. Phospholipase A2 and cholesterol esterase break down phospholipids and cholesterol esters, respectively.

Humans lack the desaturase enzymes required to introduce double bonds beyond the $\Delta^9$ position of fatty acids, making linoleic acid (LA, an omega-6 polyunsaturated fatty acid) and $\alpha$-linolenic acid (ALA, an omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acid) essential in the diet. These essential fatty acids serve as precursors for long-chain eicosanoids, docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), and eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA).

## Dietary Guidelines for Americans (2025–2030) paradigm shifts

The release of the Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2025–2030 represents a major paradigm shift, emphasizing "real food" and an inverted dietary pyramid that prioritizes high-quality protein, full-fat dairy, healthy animal and plant fats, vegetables, fruits, and whole grains. The guidelines call for a dramatic reduction in highly processed foods and refined carbohydrates, which are associated with systemic inflammation, glycemic dysregulation, and metabolic syndrome.

Key recommendations include prioritizing protein density at every meal, targeting $1.2\text{ to }1.6\text{ g/kg/day}$ of protein. Dairy consumption has shifted to encourage full-fat, unsweetened options, setting a target of 3 servings per day within a 2,000-calorie pattern.

Vegetable and fruit targets are set at 3 servings of colorful, nutrient-dense vegetables and 2 servings of whole fruits daily. Fiber-rich whole grains are prioritized, recommending 2 to 4 servings per day while reducing refined grains like white bread, flour tortillas, and crackers.

Added sugars are strictly limited to no more than 10 grams per meal. Snack foods must align with FDA "Healthy" claim limits: grain snacks must not exceed 5 grams of added sugar per 3/4 ounce whole-grain equivalent, and dairy snacks must not exceed 2.5 grams of added sugar per 2/3 cup equivalent.

The guidelines emphasize the reduction of chemical food additives and food packaging contaminants. These contaminants include substances from packaging materials that can accumulate in reproductive tissues, brains, and atherosclerotic lesions.

To support the gut microbiome, the consumption of fermented foods (e.g., sauerkraut, kimchi, kefir, miso) and high-fiber whole foods is encouraged. Sodium recommendations are detailed across age-specific limits to manage cardiovascular risks.

# Chapter 2: Micronutrient Profiles, Trace Element Biochemistry, and Clinical Deficiencies

Micronutrients do not directly provide energy, but they are indispensable coenzymes, structural components, and cellular signaling agents. Chronic inadequacy or excess leads to distinct pathophysiological states.

## Focused Pathophysiological Mechanisms of Deficiencies

The biochemical pathways of specific vitamin deficiencies explain their complex clinical presentations.

### Dry Beriberi versus Wet Beriberi

Thiamin deficiency leads to distinct neurological and cardiovascular phenotypes. Because thiamin pyrophosphate (TPP) is a required coenzyme for pyruvate dehydrogenase, $\alpha$-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, and transketolase, its absence severely halts aerobic carbohydrate metabolism, selectively injuring tissues with high energy demands. This metabolic failure leads to the accumulation of pyruvic and lactic acids in tissues and body fluids.

* **Dry Beriberi:** This presentation is characterized by symmetrical peripheral neuropathy. The pathological changes include myelin degeneration and axonal wasting without acute inflammation, targeting the lower extremities first. Patients present with paresthesias, sensory loss, diminished deep tendon reflexes, difficulty walking, and progressive muscle wasting. Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome represents a severe, central nervous system extension of dry beriberi, featuring Wernicke's encephalopathy (ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion) and Korsakoff's psychosis (antegrade amnesia, confabulation).
* **Wet Beriberi:** This presentation manifests as cardiovascular dysfunction. Impaired myocardial energy metabolism coupled with peripheral vasodilation (dysautonomia) triggers high-output congestive heart failure. Capillary walls weaken, leading to systemic fluid retention and edema. Clinical indicators include orthopnea, tachycardia, cardiomegaly, elevated pulmonary pressures, and severe bilateral lower extremity edema. If not treated rapidly with intravenous thiamin, wet beriberi can progress to cardiovascular collapse and death within hours.

### Pernicious Anemia, Intrinsic Factor, and the Vitamin $\text{B}_{12}$ Absorption Pathway

Vitamin $\text{B}_{12}$ absorption requires a sequence of highly specialized gastrointestinal steps. First, dietary $\text{B}_{12}$, which is bound to animal proteins, is released in the stomach through the proteolytic action of pepsin and hydrochloric acid. Once released, it immediately binds to haptocorrin (R-protein), a glycoprotein secreted by the salivary glands and gastric mucosa, which protects the vitamin from acid degradation.

In the alkaline environment of the duodenum, pancreatic proteases cleave haptocorrin, freeing $\text{B}_{12}$ to bind with gastric intrinsic factor (IF), a $45\text{-kDa}$ glycoprotein synthesized by the parietal cells of the gastric body and fundus. The highly stable $\text{B}_{12}$-IF complex travels to the terminal ileum, where it binds to specific cubilin-amnionless receptor complexes on the enterocyte membrane, initiating absorption via receptor-mediated endocytosis.

Pernicious anemia is an autoimmune atrophic gastritis where autoantibodies target and destroy gastric parietal cells, or directly bind to and inhibit intrinsic factor. This loss eliminates both intrinsic factor and hydrochloric acid secretion. Without these, dietary $\text{B}_{12}$ cannot be released from food or absorbed in the terminal ileum.

Over several years, as hepatic cobalamin stores are depleted, this malabsorption halts DNA synthesis, leading to megaloblastic anemia and subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. Clinical evaluation of suspected deficiency includes measuring serum $\text{B}_{12}$ and folate, along with specific markers: elevated methylmalonic acid (MMA) is highly sensitive for $\text{B}_{12}$ deficiency, while elevated homocysteine occurs in both $\text{B}_{12}$ and folate deficiencies.

The historical Schilling test, which measures the urinary excretion of radiolabeled $\text{B}_{12}$ with and without exogenous intrinsic factor, remains the classic diagnostic method to confirm the absence of functional intrinsic factor.

## Pathogenesis of Rare Mineral Deficiencies

Genetic mutations or environmental factors can result in severe mineral-deficiency phenotypes.

### Menkes Disease

Menkes disease is an X-linked recessive genetic disorder characterized by a mutation in the ATP7A gene. This gene encodes a copper-transporting P-type ATPase responsible for exporting copper across cell membranes. When ATP7A is defective, dietary copper is absorbed by enterocytes in the duodenum and jejunum but becomes trapped within their cytoplasm, unable to be exported into the portal circulation.

This entrapment leads to systemic, severe copper deficiency. Copper-dependent enzymes lose catalytic activity. The inactivation of lysyl oxidase (which crosslinks collagen and elastin) causes structural vascular defects and connective tissue abnormalities. Inactivation of dopamine $\beta$-hydroxylase halts catecholamine synthesis, while cytochrome c oxidase failure halts mitochondrial ATP generation, causing progressive neurological degeneration, temperature instability, pili torti (coarse, sparse "kinky" hair), cutis laxa, skeletal fractures, and infantile death.

### Keshan Disease

Keshan disease is a localized, endemic cardiomyopathy caused by severe dietary selenium deficiency. First identified in regions of China with selenium-depleted soil, its pathogenesis relies on the loss of glutathione peroxidase (GPx) and other antioxidant selenoproteins. Selenium deficiency severely downregulates GPx activity, disabling the clearance of lipid hydroperoxides and reactive oxygen species in myocardial mitochondria.

This elevated oxidative stress compromises cardiac mitochondrial respiration and ATP production, leading to progressive myocardial cell collapse, cell death, and subsequent fibrous remodeling of the tissue. Clinically, Keshan disease manifests as a dilated cardiomyopathy, characterized by cardiomegaly, congestive heart failure, severe cardiac arrhythmias, and a high mortality rate. A co-factor, such as an enterovirus (e.g., Coxsackievirus), often accelerates the progression of the disease under selenium-deficient conditions.

## The Ketogenic Diet

The ketogenic diet shifts the body's primary energy substrate from glucose to fat-derived ketone bodies. The drop in insulin and rise in glucagon levels activate hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL) in adipose tissue, releasing free fatty acids into circulation. These fatty acids are transported into liver mitochondria via the carnitine palmitoyltransferase system (specifically CPT-1 on the outer mitochondrial membrane) to undergo $\beta$-oxidation.

This process generates an excess of acetyl-CoA, which is shunted into ketogenesis. Two acetyl-CoA molecules are condensed by thiolase to form acetoacetyl-CoA, which HMG-CoA synthase (the rate-limiting enzyme of ketogenesis) converts into $\beta$-hydroxy-$\beta$-methylglutaryl-CoA (HMG-CoA). HMG-CoA lyase then cleaves this intermediate to yield acetoacetate. Acetoacetate can be reduced to D-$\beta$-hydroxybutyrate (the dominant circulating ketone) by D-$\beta$-hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase, or spontaneously decarboxylate into volatile acetone, which is excreted via the lungs and produces a distinct fruity breath odor.

In target tissues (such as the brain, heart, and skeletal muscle), ketones are converted back into acetyl-CoA for ATP production through ketolysis. Acetoacetate is activated to acetoacetyl-CoA by succinyl-CoA-oxoacid transferase (SCOT), using succinyl-CoA as the coenzyme A donor. This step is rate-limiting for ketone utilization.

Crucially, high concentrations of acetoacetate feed back to inhibit SCOT, preventing over-utilization. The liver lacks SCOT, preventing it from utilizing the ketones it synthesizes. Standard urinary ketone test strips utilize a nitroprusside reaction that selectively measures acetoacetate, often failing to detect D-$\beta$-hydroxybutyrate, which is the predominant ketone during severe ketoacidosis.

## Plant-Based Diets (Vegan and Vegetarian)

Vegetarian patterns exclude animal flesh but allow secondary animal products like dairy and eggs, whereas vegan diets eliminate all animal-derived foods and additives. A primary challenge of vegan diets is obtaining complete proteins.

Most plant proteins lack one or more essential amino acids, making them incomplete. For example, grains and nuts are typically low in lysine, while legumes are low in methionine and cysteine. To obtain a complete amino acid profile, plant-based diets must include complementary protein sources (such as rice and beans) within the same day.

Risks of strict vegan diets include severe Vitamin $\text{B}_{12}$ deficiency (as cobalamin is found naturally only in animal foods), zinc and iron deficiencies (due to phytates in plants reducing mineral absorption), and inadequate long-chain omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA).

## The Gluten-Free Diet

The gluten-free diet is a medical necessity for individuals with celiac disease (an autoimmune enteropathy), non-celiac gluten sensitivity, or wheat allergy. It requires the strict elimination of gluten, a structural protein composite of gliadin and glutenin found in wheat, rye, barley, and triticale.

Because gluten provides elasticity and structure in traditional baking, gluten-free baking requires alternative flours (e.g., rice, tapioca, potato, amaranth, quinoa). To compensate for the loss of gluten's binding properties, gluten-free recipes should be baked at a $25^\circ\text{F}$ ($14^\circ\text{C}$) lower oven temperature for a slightly longer duration.

Additionally, adding $1/2\text{ teaspoon}$ of baking powder per cup of flour, or incorporating an extra egg, can help gluten-free baked goods rise.

## The Low-FODMAP Diet

The low-FODMAP diet is a three-phase nutritional therapy designed for patients with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). FODMAPs are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine, draw excess water into the intestinal lumen, and undergo rapid fermentation by colonic bacteria, resulting in gas, pain, and altered motility.

* **Phase I: Elimination:** All high-FODMAP foods are restricted for two to six weeks to resolve gastrointestinal symptoms.
* **Phase II: Reintroduction:** Individual FODMAP groups are systematically reintroduced one at a time over six to eight weeks to identify specific triggers and tolerance thresholds.
* **Phase III: Personalization:** Tolerated carbohydrates are reincorporated into the diet, while only highly triggering foods are restricted long-term.

High-FODMAP groups to avoid during the elimination phase include:
* **Excess Fructose:** Apples, cherries, pears, watermelon, and honey.
* **Lactose:** Milk, yogurt, cottage cheese, and ice cream.
* **Fructans and Galacto-oligosaccharides (GOS):** Wheat, rye, barley, garlic, onions, and legumes.
* **Polyols:** Cauliflower, mushrooms, stone fruits, and artificial sweeteners like sorbitol or mannitol.

## The Low-Sodium Diet

The low-sodium diet is designed to manage hypertension, chronic kidney disease, and congestive heart failure by reducing fluid retention and peripheral vascular resistance. The general population over age 14 should consume less than $2,300\text{ mg/day}$ of sodium.

For pediatric populations, limits are adjusted by age: children aged 1 to 3 require less than $1,200\text{ mg/day}$, those aged 4 to 8 require less than $1,500\text{ mg/day}$, and those aged 9 to 13 require less than $1,800\text{ mg/day}$. Highly active individuals with high sweat losses may increase intake to offset electrolyte losses.

# Chapter 4: Food Allergen Immunology, Cross-Reactivity, and Culinary Substitutions

Food allergies are IgE-mediated immunological responses directed against specific proteins within foods. Upon ingestion, these proteins crosslink IgE antibodies bound to mast cells and basophils, triggering degranulation and the release of inflammatory mediators like histamine, which can cause symptoms ranging from localized urticaria to life-threatening anaphylaxis.

## Immunological pathways of major allergens

* **Cow's Milk Proteins:** The primary allergens are casein proteins, which make up approximately $80\%$ of total milk protein, and whey proteins (alpha-lactalbumin, beta-lactoglobulin, and bovine serum albumin). Caseins are highly heat-resistant; therefore, individuals with high levels of casein-specific IgE are more likely to react to baked milk products, whereas those with whey-specific allergies can often tolerate baked milk. Ruminant milks (goat, sheep, buffalo) exhibit over $90\%$ immunological cross-reactivity with cow's milk.
* **Hen's Egg Proteins:** Egg allergy is driven by egg-white proteins, primarily ovomucoid, ovalbumin, and ovotransferrin. Ovomucoid is highly heat-resistant, and high ovomucoid-specific IgE levels indicate a low tolerance for baked egg products. The egg-yolk protein alpha-livetin is implicated in "bird-egg syndrome," where patients exhibit cross-reactivity between bird feathers, poultry meat, and egg yolks.
* **Soy and Legumes:** Soybeans are common allergens in infants and young children. Highly refined soybean oil and soy lecithin are generally tolerated because the allergenic proteins are removed during processing. While over half of peanut-allergic individuals test positive for other legumes (such as soy), $95\%$ of them can tolerate and eat these other legumes without reaction.
* **Peanuts and Tree Nuts:** Peanuts are legumes, whereas tree nuts are distinct botanical families. However, cross-contamination during processing is common. Tree nut allergies exhibit strong botanical family cross-reactivity: over $97\%$ of pistachio-allergic individuals react to cashew, and $75\%$ to $100\%$ of pecan-allergic individuals react to walnut.
* **Shellfish and Molluscs:** The primary allergen driving crustacean allergy is tropomyosin, a highly conserved muscle protein. Tropomyosin exhibits significant cross-reactivity with house dust mites (Der p 10, Blo t 10) and cockroaches, which frequently leads to false-positive shellfish IgE blood tests in dust-mite-allergic patients. Molluscs (clams, oysters, squid) also contain tropomyosin, but cross-reactivity with crustacean tropomyosin varies.

# Chapter 5: Food Science, Thermal Kinetics, and Processing Contaminants

Cooking modifies the chemical structures of foods, improving digestability, releasing certain nutrients, and destroying pathogens. However, high-temperature cooking can also lead to the degradation of sensitive micronutrients and the formation of toxic contaminants.

## Thermal kinetics of pathogen destruction

Pathogen destruction in food is a first-order kinetic decay process governed by temperature, moisture, and time. At temperatures above $130^\circ\text{F}$ ($54^\circ\text{C}$), cellular proteins in bacteria begin to denature, but the rate of destruction varies.

For example, at $140^\circ\text{F}$ ($60^\circ\text{C}$), Salmonella populations decay at a rate of 0.1 log per minute, requiring prolonged exposure to achieve pasteurization. At $145^\circ\text{F}$ ($63^\circ\text{C}$), the decay rate accelerates to 1.8 logs per minute, and at $165^\circ\text{F}$ ($74^\circ\text{C}$), pathogen destruction is virtually instantaneous.

The Maillard browning reaction occurs at temperatures between $280^\circ\text{F}$ and $330^\circ\text{F}$ ($138^\circ\text{C}$ to $166^\circ\text{C}$), which is completely decoupled from internal temperatures. This explains why a well-seared chicken breast can still harbor live Campylobacter at its core if the center has not reached $165^\circ\text{F}$.

Additionally, consumer-grade instant-read probes can exhibit measurement errors. Testing has shown that $41\%$ of consumer probes read at least $3^\circ\text{F}$ low at $165^\circ\text{F}$, meaning a display of $165^\circ\text{F}$ could represent an actual internal temperature of $162^\circ\text{F}$, where Salmonella survives twelve times longer.

During cooking, thermal energy continues to transfer from the hot outer layers of food to the cooler center even after it is removed from the heat source. This carryover cooking varies by the food's mass and density:
* A 2-inch-thick ribeye steak resting for 5 minutes can gain $5^\circ\text{F}\text{ to }7^\circ\text{F}$ ($3^\circ\text{C}\text{ to }4^\circ\text{C}$) in its center.
* A 6-pound whole chicken resting for 20 minutes can gain $10^\circ\text{F}\text{ to }15^\circ\text{F}$ ($6^\circ\text{C}\text{ to }8^\circ\text{C}$).
* A thin 1/2-inch pork chop gains only $2^\circ\text{F}\text{ to }3^\circ\text{F}$ ($1^\circ\text{C}$), requiring it to be cooked closer to its target temperature before removal.

Microwaving raw meat can be safe if done properly, but microwaves generate significant cold spots. This can be remediated by stirring or rotating the food halfway through the cooking process, covering the container to trap steam (which raises the ambient temperature), and verifying the final temperature in three separate locations.

## Nutrient retention chemistry

Nutrient retention during cooking depends on the physical and chemical changes in the food matrix. These changes are classified into three types:
* **Type 1 Changes (Volatiles Lost):** Soluble solids are retained, but volatile components and moisture are lost, such as when vegetables are steamed or peanuts are oven-roasted. True retention of vitamins under these conditions is high, and differences between apparent and true retention are minimal.
* **Type 2 Changes (Moisture Gained):** Food absorbs water without losing solids, such as when rice is boiled and absorbs all the cooking water. In this type, apparent retention can underestimate true retention due to the dilution of nutrients by water weight.
* **Type 3 Changes (Solids Lost, Moisture Gained):** Soluble solids leach into the cooking water while moisture is gained, such as when dry legumes are simmered and the cooking liquid is discarded. This leads to significant losses of water-soluble vitamins and minerals.

Adding salt during cooking has a more significant impact on the final sodium level of the food than the cooking method itself. However, sodium uptake can be reduced by pre-soaking the food in water or using an excess of cooking water to dilute the sodium concentration.

## Mitigation of processing contaminants

High-temperature cooking can generate toxic, mutagenic, and carcinogenic compounds.

### Acrylamide

Acrylamide is a probable human carcinogen (Group 2A) that forms when the amino acid asparagine reacts with reducing sugars (such as glucose or fructose) via the Maillard reaction at temperatures above $248^\circ\text{F}$ ($120^\circ\text{C}$). It is commonly found in fried potato products, roasted coffee, toasted bread, and baked goods.

To mitigate acrylamide formation:
* **Soak Potatoes:** Soaking potato slices in cold water for 15 to 30 minutes before frying leaches out free sugars and asparagine from the surface, reducing acrylamide formation.
* **Avoid Cold Storage:** Do not store potatoes below $43^\circ\text{F}$ ($6^\circ\text{C}$). Cold storage triggers "cold-induced sweetening," which hydrolyzes starch into glucose and fructose, increasing acrylamide precursors during cooking.
* **Enzymatic Depletion:** Commercial food manufacturers use the enzyme asparaginase to convert asparagine into aspartic acid, preventing the reaction without affecting flavor or browning.
* **Adjust Cooking Methods:** Opt for steaming, boiling, or microwaving over deep frying, roasting, or grilling, as lower temperatures and water-based methods generate minimal acrylamide.

### Heterocyclic Amines (HCAs) and Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs)

* **Heterocyclic Amines (HCAs):** HCAs form when amino acids, sugars, and creatine or creatinine in muscle meats react at high temperatures ($212^\circ\text{F}\text{ to }572^\circ\text{F}$ / $100^\circ\text{C}\text{ to }300^\circ\text{C}$), such as during pan-frying or grilling.
* **Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs):** PAHs form when meat fat drips onto open flames or hot coals, generating volatile compounds in the smoke that coat the surface of the food.

**Mitigation:** Marinating meats in polyphenol-rich marinades (containing garlic, rosemary, turmeric, or beer) can reduce HCA formation by $60\%$ to $70\%$. However, marinades containing milk or added sugars can increase surface acrylamide levels, requiring careful selection based on the food matrix. Removing drippings, minimizing smoke, and avoiding charring also significantly lower PAH exposure.
